BERT vs Generative AI for Text Classification

Comparing transformer-based classification (BERT) with generative AI approaches for NLP text analysis tasks in a Jupyter notebook environment.

Project Overview

This project was completed as part of my MSc in Business Analytics at Dublin City University. The assessment involved working in a Jupyter notebook environment to implement and evaluate two distinct approaches to text classification: a traditional BERT-based transformer model and a Generative AI approach, comparing their performance, workflow characteristics, and practical trade-offs.

Background

Text classification is a foundational NLP task with wide business applications — from sentiment analysis of customer feedback to automated document routing, spam filtering, and topic categorisation. Two major paradigms have emerged for tackling these tasks:

  1. BERT-style discriminative models — fine-tuned transformer encoders that learn to classify input text directly into predefined categories. Well-suited to structured, supervised classification with labelled training data.

  2. Generative AI approaches — large language models prompted to perform classification through instruction following, few-shot prompting, or zero-shot reasoning. Require no fine-tuning but rely on prompt design and model capability.

Comparing these approaches — not just on accuracy, but on interpretability, resource requirements, workflow design, and business fit — was the core analytical challenge of this project.

Methodology

BERT-Based Classification

The BERT pipeline involved:

  • Tokenisation using the Hugging Face transformers library (bert-base-uncased)
  • Fine-tuning on a labelled text dataset with a classification head
  • Evaluation using accuracy, F1 score, and confusion matrix analysis
  • Training via Google Colab with GPU runtime to manage compute requirements

Generative AI Approach

The generative AI pipeline involved:

  • Zero-shot and few-shot prompting to elicit classifications from a large language model
  • Prompt engineering to structure inputs consistently and extract structured label outputs
  • Evaluation on the same held-out test set for direct comparison with BERT

Key Findings

Dimension BERT Generative AI
Accuracy (test set) Strong on in-distribution data Competitive on ambiguous cases
Training required Yes — labelled data needed No — zero-shot capable
Interpretability Moderate (attention weights) Low (black-box prompting)
Latency Fast at inference Slower per call
Workflow complexity Higher (fine-tuning pipeline) Lower (prompt-based)
Best fit High-volume, structured tasks Exploratory or low-resource settings

The comparison revealed that model choice is not just an accuracy decision — it is a workflow design decision that depends on the volume of labelled data available, the required inference speed, the interpretability demands of the use case, and the cost of model deployment.

Reflection

This project had a significant impact on how I think about model selection in applied NLP.

Going into it, I expected the comparison to be straightforward: BERT would win on accuracy for a structured classification task, and generative AI would be less precise but more flexible. What I found was more nuanced. Generative AI approaches performed surprisingly well on ambiguous or edge-case examples where context and semantic understanding mattered more than pattern matching on labelled training examples. BERT, by contrast, was more consistent and reliable on clear, in-distribution cases where sufficient labelled data had been provided.

More importantly, I came to understand that the framing of “which model is better” is often the wrong question. The better question is: better for what, in what context, with what constraints? A business deploying a customer service routing tool at scale needs speed, consistency, and low operational cost — BERT fine-tuned on their specific categories is likely the right tool. An analyst quickly exploring the themes in a new corpus of documents, without labelled data, may get more value from a generative AI approach in hours rather than weeks.

Working inside a Jupyter notebook also reinforced the importance of clear, reproducible experimental structure. Tracking experiments, documenting preprocessing decisions, and presenting evaluation results in a readable format are skills I actively practised and improved through this project.

The project deepened my interest in NLP and applied machine learning considerably. It also gave me a much more grounded understanding of the practical engineering trade-offs involved in deploying language models — something that is increasingly relevant for anyone working in business analytics as AI tools become standard components of the analytics stack.
